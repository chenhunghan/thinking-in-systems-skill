# Phase 1: Is It a System?

Before modeling anything, establish that we're looking at a system — not just a collection of parts.

## The four tests

Help the user work through these questions:

### a. Can you identify the parts (elements)?

What are the things in this situation? People, resources, processes, information, physical objects, organizations. List them — they're the easiest part of a system to see. But don't stop here. Elements are the most replaceable part of a system. The system's behavior usually doesn't depend on specific elements — it depends on how they're connected.

If the user has already listed elements, confirm what they've named and then ask: "Are there any parts you might be leaving out? Sometimes the less visible elements — information, norms, or feedback channels — matter most."

### b. Do the parts affect each other (interconnections)?

This is harder to see than elements. Interconnections can be physical flows (money, materials, energy) or information flows (signals, decisions, rules, policies). Ask:
- "If this part changed, what else would change?"
- "How does information move between these parts?"
- "What rules or policies govern how these parts interact?"

Interconnections are often more important than elements. Changing the players rarely changes the game — changing the rules does.

### c. Do the parts together produce an effect different from each part on its own (emergence)?

A pile of car parts is not a car. A collection of musicians is not an orchestra. Does the whole do something that no individual part can do alone? What is that emergent effect?

If you can't identify emergence, this may not be a system — it may just be a collection.

### d. Does the behavior persist over time in a variety of circumstances?

If you replaced individual elements, would the system still behave similarly? A sports team can lose players and still play like the same team. A company can have complete employee turnover and maintain its culture. If the behavior comes from the structure rather than from particular elements, you're looking at a system.

## Identifying the three key components

Once the four tests suggest yes, name the three components explicitly:

1. **Elements** — the visible, tangible parts (easiest to see)
2. **Interconnections** — how elements affect each other (harder to see)
3. **Function or purpose** — what the system actually does (hardest to see, most important)

Spend extra time on function or purpose. Ask the user: "What does this system *do*? Not what is it *supposed* to do — but what does it *actually* do?" The gap between stated purpose and actual behavior is often where the most important insights live. A system that claims to "improve education" but actually sorts students by socioeconomic status has a very different actual purpose than its stated one.

## If it's not clearly a system

That's a useful finding. Not everything is a system. If the parts don't interconnect meaningfully or don't produce emergent behavior, a systems lens may not be the right tool here. Help the user see this clearly rather than forcing a systems frame where it doesn't fit. That honesty is itself part of good systems thinking.

## Moving forward

When elements, interconnections, and function/purpose are identified, you have the foundation. Move to Phase 2 to find the stocks and flows — the dynamic structure that produces the behavior you observe.
