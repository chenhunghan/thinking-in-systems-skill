# Phase 6: System Traps and Opportunities

System traps are common structural patterns that produce problematic behavior. They are "traps" because the structure makes the behavior feel impossible to escape — the harder people try within the existing structure, the worse things get. But every trap has a corresponding opportunity: a structural shift that can release the system.

The trap is not the behavior — it's the structure that produces the behavior. Recognizing the trap means seeing the structure, and that's where the opportunity lives.

## The eight traps

### Policy resistance

**Signature:** Multiple actors pull the system toward different goals. Every effort by one actor to change the system is offset by other actors pushing back. Everyone works harder and harder, but the system barely moves — or oscillates around a compromise that nobody wants.

**Why it happens:** Several balancing loops are attached to the same stock, each trying to pull it toward a different goal. The stock settles at a weighted average. Any actor who pushes harder triggers compensating counterpush from the others.

**Opportunity:** Stop pulling against each other. Find a goal that all actors can align on, or let go of the tug-of-war. Redefine success at the system level rather than the actor level. Sometimes this means bringing the actors together to see the whole system — when everyone sees the structure, the futility of pulling harder becomes obvious.

### Tragedy of the commons

**Signature:** A shared resource (a common stock) is depleted as each user acts in their own rational self-interest. Each individual gain comes at a small cost distributed across everyone, but the accumulated costs destroy the shared resource.

**Why it happens:** There is no feedback loop — or the feedback is too weak or too delayed — connecting individual use to the state of the commons. Each user sees only the benefit of their use, not their share of the cumulative damage.

**Opportunity:** Create the missing feedback. Make the costs of overuse visible and immediate to each user. Options include: regulating access, dividing the commons into private shares, or creating social norms and mutual monitoring that connect use to consequence.

### Drift to low performance

**Signature:** Standards gradually erode. "Good enough" slides downward as each small shortfall becomes the new normal. The system slowly degrades and no one sounds the alarm because each individual step is too small to notice.

**Why it happens:** The goal or standard is anchored to past performance rather than to an absolute benchmark. When performance drops a little, the goal drops to match ("well, this is where we are now"). The balancing loop that should correct performance instead adjusts the goal. It's a reinforcing loop of declining expectations.

**Opportunity:** Anchor the standard to an absolute or external benchmark, not to recent performance. Make the standard visible and non-negotiable. Compare performance to the best that has been achieved or to where you want to be, not to where you were last month.

### Escalation

**Signature:** Two or more actors each respond to the other's actions by doing more. Arms races, price wars, advertising battles, shouting matches. Each action triggers a reaction that triggers a stronger action.

**Why it happens:** A reinforcing loop connects the actors — each side's state is a stock that drives the other's flow upward. There's no balancing loop to cap the escalation. Each actor's local rationality says "I must match or exceed what they did."

**Opportunity:** Refuse to compete — unilateral de-escalation. Or negotiate limits — arms control treaties, price agreements, ceasefire. Or restructure so the actors are no longer in a zero-sum competitive loop. The hardest part is that the first actor to de-escalate feels like they're losing.

### Success to the successful

**Signature:** Winners get more resources, which helps them win more, which gets them more resources. Losers get fewer resources and fall further behind. Inequality compounds over time. Two (or more) actors start with a small difference and end with a chasm.

**Why it happens:** A reinforcing loop where success increases access to the means of further success. No balancing loop redistributes or rebalances. The rich get richer, the poor get poorer — not through malice but through structure.

**Opportunity:** Diversify the system so that success in one arena doesn't automatically confer advantage in all arenas. Create balancing mechanisms (progressive taxation, anti-monopoly rules, rotation of roles, ensuring universal access to basic means of success). Or periodically level the playing field.

### Shifting the burden to the intervenor

**Signature:** An external intervention relieves a symptom, which reduces the pressure to fix the underlying problem. Over time, the system becomes dependent on the intervention and loses its own ability to self-correct. The intervenor becomes trapped — they can't stop without the system collapsing.

**Why it happens:** The intervention activates a fast balancing loop (symptom relief) that weakens a slower balancing loop (the system's own self-correction). Over time, the self-correction capacity atrophies from disuse. Like muscles that weaken when you use a crutch too long.

**Opportunity:** Intervene in a way that strengthens the system's own ability to fix itself. Use external support as a bridge to self-sufficiency, not as a permanent crutch. Build in a sunset: the intervention should phase out as the system's own capacity grows.

### Rule beating

**Signature:** People follow the letter of the rules while violating their spirit. The rules produce perverse behavior because they measure or constrain the wrong thing. People optimize for the metric, not for the purpose.

**Why it happens:** The rules create a balancing loop around a proxy metric that doesn't actually align with the system's real purpose. People are smart — they optimize for what is measured and rewarded. If the measurement is wrong, the optimization goes wrong too.

**Opportunity:** Redesign the rules to align with the actual purpose. Measure outcomes, not proxies. Or better yet: create conditions where the desired behavior emerges naturally without needing rules to enforce it. If people are gaming the rules, the rules are probably measuring the wrong thing.

### Seeking the wrong goal

**Signature:** The system faithfully and efficiently pursues a goal — but the goal doesn't actually lead to what people want. The system works perfectly. It's just working perfectly toward the wrong thing.

**Why it happens:** The defined goal (often a measurable metric) has diverged from the actual desired outcome (often harder to measure). GDP grows but well-being declines. Test scores rise but learning doesn't. The system optimizes for what it can count.

**Opportunity:** Redefine the goal. This is one of the highest-leverage interventions available, but also one of the hardest — measurable proxies are tempting because they feel objective. The work is to find goals that actually represent what matters, even if they're harder to quantify. If you can't measure what you value, you'll end up valuing what you can measure.

## How to use this phase

Don't force-fit traps. Match from the structure you've already mapped in Phases 1-3, not from surface symptoms or keyword matching. Ask:
- "Does the structural signature of any trap match what we see in our model?"
- "Are there feedback loops in our diagram that match a trap's pattern?"
- "Could more than one trap be active simultaneously?"

When you recognize a trap:
1. Name it — but as "this looks like..." not "this is..."
2. Show the user how the trap's structural signature maps to their specific system
3. Discuss the opportunity — the structural change that could release the trap
4. Ask: "Does this match what you observe? Does it explain anything that puzzled you?"

A system may exhibit multiple traps simultaneously, or none of the common ones. The traps are patterns to recognize, not a checklist to complete.

## Moving forward

With traps and their opportunities identified, move to Phase 7 — leverage points — for a systematic framework for thinking about where in the system intervention has the most power.
